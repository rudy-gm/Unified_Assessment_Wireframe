The regulatory convergence challenge

Four guidelines. One enterprise risk problem.

Click each guideline to understand what banks must do, why it matters, and how it connects to the others.

OSFI B-10
Third-Party Risk Management
Effective May 1, 2024
Purpose

Assessing and managing risks that arise from third parties — outsourcing, cloud providers, data suppliers, and intra-group arrangements.

Banks must
  • Maintain a comprehensive TPRM framework
  • Perform risk-based due diligence on all third parties
  • Conduct ongoing monitoring proportionate to criticality
  • Establish credible exit plans for critical arrangements
Why it matters
Outsourced Risk — Loss of direct control, hidden fourth-party risk. Third-party risk is now enterprise concentration risk.
Connects to

B-13 (technology risks from vendors) · E-21 (critical operations dependencies) · E-23 (vendor AI and model risk)

OSFI B-13
Technology & Cyber Risk Management
Effective Jan 1, 2024
Purpose

Governance and management of cybersecurity and technology risk — from IT asset management through cyber incident response and DR testing.

Banks must
  • Maintain a technology and cyber risk framework
  • Protect systems with effective security controls
  • Manage risks across the full technology lifecycle
  • Test DR scenarios against severe-but-plausible events
Why it matters
Increasing external dependency — cloud providers and fintech partnerships create systemic technology fragility.
Connects to

B-10 (technology risks from third parties) · E-21 (technology as critical operation dependency) · E-23 (AI model infrastructure)

OSFI E-21
Operational Risk & Resilience
Effective Aug 2024 · Full Sep 2026
Purpose

Managing and building resilience around operational risks — from governance and risk appetite through critical operation testing and business continuity.

Banks must
  • Maintain an operational risk and resilience framework
  • Map and manage risks to critical operations
  • Define measurable disruption tolerances
  • Ensure tested, demonstrable business continuity
Why it matters
Disruptions to critical services — cyber-attacks, vendor failures, model errors cascade into enterprise-wide resilience failures.
Connects to

B-10 (third-party dependencies) · B-13 (technology as critical dependency) · E-23 (model-dependent operations)

OSFI E-23
Model Risk & AI Governance
Effective May 1, 2027 — begin now
Purpose

Managing risk arising from the use of risk models and AI/ML systems — covering inventory, validation, lifecycle governance, and third-party AI oversight.

Banks must
  • Maintain a model risk and AI management framework
  • Keep a risk-tiered model inventory including AI
  • Govern models across their full lifecycle
  • Conduct independent validation proportionate to risk
Why it matters
Increased complexity — Gen AI, automation, and advanced analytics are outpacing governance maturity at most institutions.
Connects to

B-10 (vendor AI as third-party risk) · B-13 (model infrastructure) · E-21 (model-dependent critical operations)

What happens if convergence is not addressed at enterprise level

If convergence is not addressed
  • 01
    Fragmentation becomes visible under scrutiny
    OSFI examiners approach the institution as an integrated enterprise — not four separate programs. When cross-domain coherence is absent, findings multiply and systemic gaps are identified that individual teams cannot see or own.
  • 02
    Remediation cycles accelerate and compound
    Siloed findings repeat because the root cause is structural — not technical. Each supervisory cycle generates new obligations on top of unresolved prior findings, creating a compounding remediation burden that consumes delivery capacity.
  • 03
    Innovation outpaces governance
    AI deployment, cloud migration, and fintech partnerships continue at pace while fragmented governance programs lag behind. The gap between what the institution is deploying and what it can demonstrate control over widens with every new use case.
  • 04
    Compliance cost becomes unsustainable
    Four separate program teams, four sets of governance committees, four reporting frameworks, and four tooling stacks — with significant duplication. As guidelines layer and supervisory expectations increase, the marginal cost of siloed compliance compounds year over year.
To respond effectively, leadership must
  • 01
    Aggregate risk enterprise-wide — Build a single view of technology, resilience, third-party, and AI/model exposure at the enterprise level — not domain by domain.
  • 02
    Operationalize measurable tolerances — Convert regulatory expectations into governance mechanisms and controls with explicit, measurable disruption tolerances.
  • 03
    Scale governance across domains — A single, proportionate governance architecture that spans all four guidelines — reducing duplication and increasing board confidence.
  • 04
    Align enterprise execution — Align regulatory, technology, and operational initiatives into a coordinated roadmap — not competing workstreams.
